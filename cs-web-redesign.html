Credit Sesame 2.0

I worked with another product designer, Josephina Kaiser, and a cross-functional team in redesigning the web app within 4.5 months. The project goals include designing a more user-centric experience, and updating the UI to reflect the new rebrand.

The current state

Credit Sesame allows users to check their credit score, and provides tips on how they can improve their score.  

Unfortunately, Credit Sesame’s existing web app causes a lot of pain to users. They are often frustrated that the app focuses too much on selling credit cards, which leads to mistrust. Moreover, product teams have primarily optimized only the overview page to generate more revenue because the data shows low engagement on other pages.

While the existing web app meets the business needs, it overlooks the user needs.

Understanding the customers

To address the user needs, we chatted with our UX Researcher, Angela Ren, who has done extensive research on the existing web app. We learned from Angela that both first-time and returning users have similar needs. They want to know:

  • what’s their credit score
  • what’s impacting their score/what caused their score to change
  • what can they do about it

We also learned that on mobile web, the engagement on other internal pages was low due to a usability issue. This led to 2 navigation paths that users tend to take, and both paths only partially address their needs:

  • Swiping the carousel to see an analysis of their credit score, which allows them to navigate to other pages of the product. This path helps users understand what’s impacting their credit score.
  • Scrolling down to see a list of actions they can take, thus staying on the overview page. This path provides actions users can take to address their credit score.

A new canvas

Building a design system

Because we were designing from scratch and engineering was migrating over to React, we saw this as an opportunity to build out a reusable component library that reflected the new rebrand. I created a Sketch library documenting the foundations and components with the help of Josephina. You can read more about my responsibilities in Design Systems.

Consolidating information architecture

Keeping Angela’s insights and recommendations in mind, Josephina and I first began rethinking the information architecture. We identified pages that could be merged, and conducted several tests. From our user testings with 29 participants, we learned:

  • 75% of testers found tab navigation to be more intuitive
  • 93% of testers successfully found credit card offers in the “Marketplace” tab
  • showing credit factors immediately on Overview vs. on a separate page called Analysis didn’t seem to impact testers’ experiences
  • testers had an easier time finding the profile page when it was in the menu than when it was outside

Balancing user and business needs

Based on our learnings, we settled on a tab navigation and having credit factors on the overview page to reduce the number of clicks for the user. Laying out the information on Overview immediately addresses the questions: “What is my credit score?” and “What’s impacting my score?”

Josephina and I addressed “what can I do [to improve my score]?” by having tips/actions within each credit factor page. We hypothesized users would most likely take action once they have context.

Because tips/actions are often tied with offers from our partners (this is how the company gains revenue), internal folks raised concerns that the lack of actions on the overview page might negatively impact revenue. Josephina and I needed to figure out how to balance user and business needs, so we revisited our designs.

We sought Angela’s help to run a usability test on two explorations—option A: Overview with multiple actions and option B: Overview with one action displaying offers. Based on the test results, we went with option A because it caused less confusion than option B. Option A also piqued the participants’ curiosity, and we saw users clicking on each action to learn more.

Challenges

Now I wish I could say that we followed this framework for all the features scoped for MVP, but unfortunately we were met with many hurdles.

Seed of doubt

About a month into the project, leadership felt we were making too many changes, and became doubtful of the success this project would bring. As a team, we relentlessly advocated what we were doing was in the best interest of the user, and that our primary KPI, retention, would bring in just as much revenue, if not more.

Lack of resources

We requested hiring a contract illustrator to assist us in creating an illustration system that aligns with the rebrand, but this never came to fruition. Josephina ended up assuming the role after I facilitated conversations in defining our illustration style. I also helped illustrate as well.

Time constraints

Josephina and I were working in 1-week sprint cadences, and development often caught up with product and design. The demand to churn out mocks prevented us from evaluating and testing a lot of our design decisions (we requested Angela to come to our team full-time but to no avail). We acknowledged our shortcomings, and created a list of features and pages we wanted to revisit post-MVP launch.

The lack of time also prevented us from clearly outlining the expected interactions in our initial design-engineering handoffs. I created a prototype using Figma and mapped out the entire MVP user flow to help engineering and QA understand at a high level the redesigned experience.

Results

After 4 and a half months, we shipped Credit Sesame 2.0 MVP in mid November 2019. We received positive feedback from our users, saying that the experience is intuitive, simple, and feels personalized. Comparing the redesign with the existing web app, we saw an increase:

Conversion

+14%

Retention

+6.3%

AVg. Revenue Per User

+3%

Takeaways

Throughout this project, I learned there is a delicate balance between business and user needs. As a product designer, I need to acknowledge the business needs, otherwise it’s a constant battle to get buy-in from leadership.

Next Steps

  • Feature parity so we can retire the old web app
  • Revisiting some flows and conducting usability testing and iterating based on feedback
  • Adding personalization to better help our users improve their credit score
